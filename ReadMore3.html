Theoretical Framework

Stuart Hall’s encoding/decoding model emphasized that media texts are encoded with dominant meanings by the producer but can be decoded differently by audiences depending on their social position (Hall, 1980). In El Salvador, encodes messages of safety, prosperity, and cultural pride. Yet the audiences, particularly Salvadorans abroad, decode these texts as selective propaganda, recognizing what is being left out of these advertisements. Hall’s framework acknowledges the gap between official narratives and lived realities, displaying the meaning is never fixed but negotiated (Hall, 1980).

Roland Barthes’ concept builds on this, by displaying how cultural texts can transform historical realities into myths that appear like the lived reality (Barthes, 1972). Tourism campaigns in El Salvador present beaches, hospitality, and increased safety as features of their national identity. But in doing so, they erase the country's historical identity and rebuild this new image of what it is to be Salvadoran. Through Barthes’ theory, safety itself becomes a myth and is commodified as a product for tourists. This myth-making process is branded through selective images and video being presented to the media, while silencing others.

Similar to Nancy Fraser’s argument of the public sphere, dominant publics often silence counterpublic voices, particularly those of marginalized groups (Fraser, 1990). Bukele’s branding constructs a strong public narrative, but ignores perspectives of human rights abuses or inequality from these spaces. Using Fraser’s framework builds Hall’s idea of decoding tourism campaigns as propaganda—advertisements can be political tools to shape who gets to speak and whose voices are erased.

However, this built identity is not purely created by the government. Tiziana Terranova’s idea of free labour highlights how digital platforms rely on unpaid cultural content by users (Terranova, 2000). Salvadorans contribute to this rebranding of national identity by posting images, videos, and stories on social media. Through their everyday life they are amplifying official campaigns, blurring the line between state propaganda and organic promotion. Terranova’s framework is especially relevant to our media representation today, where national identity is co-produced by governments and citizens.

This advertising builds on the perception Bukele wants a Salvadoran to have—national pride for their country. In El Salvador, safety and national pride are packaged into tourism efforts, but in the media this hides the lived realities of Salvadorans’ everyday life. Karl Marx’s idea of commodity fetishism frames tourism branding as commodification. He argues that commodities hide the social relations that produce them (Marx, 1867). This is seen through the branding of El Salvador and how it transforms lived experiences into consumable images for an international audience.
